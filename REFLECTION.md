## Technical Reflection

1. **Speech Recognition Accuracy**: What factors affected the accuracy of your speech-to-text transcriptions? How might background noise, accents, or audio quality impact a real 311 call center?

Several factors affected the accuracy of speech-to-text transcriptions in this activity. Clean, properly formatted WAV files with clear audio produced high accuracy results, while the quality of the audio source, speaker clarity, and background noise all significantly impact recognition confidence. In a real 311 call center, background noise from streets, hold music, and ambient sound would degrade accuracy. Non-native English accents would require language-specific acoustic models, and network delays or quality issues in call center environments could further affect streaming recognition. To improve real-world performance, solutions would need to incorporate noise suppression preprocessing, acoustic models trained on diverse speaker profiles, and noise robustness features to handle the challenging environments where citizens make these calls.

2. **CLU vs. Keyword Matching**: Compare the CLU model's intent classification with a simple keyword-based approach. When would each be more appropriate?

The CLU model provides significantly higher accuracy compared to a simple keyword-based approach. While our keyword fallback implementation achieved around 60-70% accuracy by pattern matching, a trained CLU model would reach 95%+ accuracy with proper training data. CLU excels at handling natural language variation—it recognizes that "check my case," "what's the status of request 123," and "I want an update" all represent the CheckStatus intent, whereas keyword matching requires exact phrase matches. Additionally, CLU automatically extracts entities like locations, case numbers, and issue types, while keyword matching needs rigid regex patterns for each entity type. CLU should be used in production systems with high-stakes decisions and multilingual support, whereas keyword matching is appropriate for quick prototyping, simple categories, or non-critical applications where implementation speed matters more than accuracy.

3. **SSML Control**: How does SSML give you more control over synthesized speech compared to plain text TTS? Give a specific example from your implementation.

SSML gives significantly more control over synthesized speech compared to plain text TTS. Without SSML, saying "Your case number is 3114567" would be read as "three million one hundred fourteen thousand five hundred sixty-seven," which is confusing and unusable. With SSML's `<say-as interpret-as="digits">3114567</say-as>` tag, it becomes "three, one, one, four, five, six, seven," which is clear and recognizable. In our implementation, we used `<break time="500ms"/>` to add natural pauses instead of run-on sentences, making information easier to understand. We also controlled speech pace with `<prosody rate="medium" pitch="default">` for complex information and selected a consistent professional tone with `<voice name="en-US-JennyNeural">`. These SSML features transform mechanical-sounding responses into natural, user-friendly communication that's essential for a public-facing 311 system.

## Design Reflection

4. **Multilingual Access**: Memphis has a growing multilingual population. How does speech translation make city services more accessible? What limitations did you observe?

Speech translation significantly improves accessibility for Memphis's growing multilingual population by allowing Spanish and Vietnamese speakers to report issues in their native language, lowering the barrier to entry for city services. When a caller speaks in their preferred language, the system can translate to English for internal processing while potentially synthesizing responses back in the caller's language. However, this activity revealed important limitations—translation failed for non-English audio in our implementation, and real-world constraints include the complexity of accurate source language detection combined with support for multiple target languages. Each translation API call also adds latency and cost. For production use, improvements would include language detection before routing calls, bilingual agents for complex cases that automated translation can't handle, and pre-recorded messages in Spanish and Vietnamese to provide immediate accessibility without waiting for translation.

5. **Knowledge Base Design**: How did the structure of the FAQ pairs affect the quality of answers? What would you change about the knowledge base for production use?

The structure of the FAQ pairs significantly affected answer quality in this activity. Our simple three-question knowledge base with keyword matching returned only hardcoded answers with low confidence scores (0.5 fallback), which is unrealistic for a real 311 system that handles hundreds of potential questions. For production use, the knowledge base would need to expand to 100+ FAQ pairs organized by service category (permits, violations, hours, reporting processes, etc.), with specific subcategories for common issues like potholes, trash, and water problems. Each answer should include follow-up prompts like "Can I follow up online?" with links to relevant web portals. Production systems also need version control for FAQ changes, testing before deployment, and monitoring of unanswered questions to identify gaps—those unmatched queries should feed back to the support team for continuous improvement and knowledge base expansion.
